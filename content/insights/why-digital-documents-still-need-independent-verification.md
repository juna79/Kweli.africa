There is a quiet assumption behind a lot of document handling: that once something exists as a PDF, an emailed scan, or a downloadable certificate, it is somehow more trustworthy than a paper original. It looks cleaner, it's easier to share, and it feels less like the kind of document that gets forged in a back room. None of that makes it more verifiable.

A digital document is, from a trust perspective, just as editable as a physical one — often more so. Changing a figure on a scanned repair estimate, editing text on a certificate, or regenerating a PDF with one field altered takes common software and a few minutes. The result looks identical to the original in every way that matters to a human glancing at it, because the tools used to view a digital document have no way of showing whether it matches what was first issued.

**Digital format solves distribution. It does not solve verification.** Those are two different problems that are easy to conflate, because moving to digital documents genuinely did fix a real issue — documents can now be shared instantly, stored cheaply, and accessed from anywhere. None of that required, or created, any independent way to confirm a specific document hasn't changed since it was issued.

If anything, the ease of editing digital files makes independent verification more necessary, not less. A paper document at least requires physical access and some effort to alter convincingly. A digital one requires neither, and the alteration leaves no visible trace in the file itself.

The fix is not to distrust digital documents or go back to paper — it is to give digital documents the one thing they were never designed to have: an independent record, created at the moment of issuance, that anyone holding the document later can check it against. Format was never the source of trust. A verifiable record of the original always was.
