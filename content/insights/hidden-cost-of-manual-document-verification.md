Ask almost any operations team what slows down a document-heavy process, and the honest answer is rarely the core decision itself. It is the confirmation work surrounding it: the second phone call to make sure a repair estimate hasn't changed, the email to a registrar's office that goes unanswered for days, the re-request for a document because a scan looked slightly off. None of that work adds value to the decision being made. All of it exists purely because there was no independent way to trust the document the first time.

This cost is genuinely difficult to see, because it never appears as its own line item. It is absorbed into headcount that exists partly to make confirmation calls, into cycle times that quietly include days of waiting on a third party to respond, into "the way this process has always worked" — which usually means nobody has separated the actual decision-making from the manual trust-building that surrounds it.

**Manual verification also does not scale evenly.** A team that can handle confirmation calls comfortably at low volume finds the same process becomes the binding constraint the moment volume grows, because adding people to make phone calls has a ceiling that adding capacity elsewhere does not. Growth in a document-heavy business often means growth in exactly the kind of manual work nobody budgeted for.

The alternative is not to remove verification — it is to move it from a manual, per-document task to something that happens automatically, the moment a document is checked. If a document's fingerprint was registered when it was issued, confirming it hasn't changed since takes seconds and requires no phone call, no email, and no dependency on whether the original issuer happens to be reachable that day.

The work that remains — the actual assessment, the actual decision — was never the slow part to begin with. Removing the manual confirmation around it simply lets that work happen at the speed it was always capable of.
