The way documents move has changed faster than the way they get verified. A degree certificate issued in one country is routinely presented to an employer in another. A shipment's paperwork passes through exporters, freight forwarders, customs officials and banks who have never dealt with each other before and may never deal with each other again. A claim can involve a garage, a hospital, an assessor and an insurer, none of whom share a system.

Each of those handoffs used to happen locally, between parties who often did know each other, or at least operated within the same regulatory and institutional context. That is increasingly not how business works. Organisations now routinely trust documents from issuers they have no relationship with, in jurisdictions they don't operate in, through channels — email, WhatsApp, scanned PDFs — that make alteration trivially easy and detection almost impossible by eye.

**Verification has not scaled with this shift.** The default method for confirming a document is still, in most industries, a phone call or an email to the original issuer — a method built for a world where the parties involved were more likely to know each other, and where volume was low enough that manual checks were tolerable. Neither of those conditions holds anymore.

The gap this creates is not hypothetical. It shows up as delayed hiring decisions while an employer waits on a registrar's office, as claims that sit in review because a report needs manual confirmation, as trade finance that moves slower than the goods it is financing because nobody downstream can quickly confirm a certificate of origin is genuine.

None of this requires new fraud techniques to become a problem — it is already a problem, created simply by more documents moving between more strangers, faster, than the verification infrastructure was ever designed to handle. Closing that gap is not a matter of working harder at the existing process. It requires a way to verify a document that does not depend on knowing, or reaching, whoever issued it.
